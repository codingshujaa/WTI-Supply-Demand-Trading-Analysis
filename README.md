# WTI Crude Oil Trading Simulation

This project is a market analysis and trading simulation built around **WTI crude oil**. The goal was to study the physical and financial drivers of the WTI market, form a market view from real supply and demand data, and then turn that view into trade ideas with clear risk and position management.


---

## Project Summary

This project focuses on one main question:

**How can supply-demand analysis in WTI crude oil be turned into a practical trading view and executed through structured trade ideas?**

To answer that, the project looks at the WTI market from both a **fundamental** and **trading** perspective. It studies how supply, demand, storage, exports, imports, pipelines, and outside market events can affect crude balances and price direction.

From that work, the team developed a **bearish view on flat price**. The analysis suggested that U.S. crude was moving into an **oversupplied market**, with supply sitting above demand, several Cushing builds showing up, and little in the near-term backdrop to create a strong bullish reversal.

That market view was then translated into actual trade ideas, mainly through:

- short flat price exposure
- time spread positions
- relative value exposure through the WTI-Brent spread

## Market View and Core Thesis

The project’s central view was that the market was leaning **bearish**.

The supply-demand work suggested that supply was above demand into late 2025, which pointed toward:

- inventory builds
- downside or sideways price action
- less chance of a strong rally

The project also noted that imports were holding up, exports were softer in some weeks, and Cushing inventories were showing builds. Taken together, that supported the idea that the market was oversupplied and that short exposure made sense.

This is one of the strongest parts of the project because it does not just say “oil might go down.” It explains **why** that view existed using actual market drivers.

---

## Trade Expression

Once the team formed the bearish view, the project moved into **trade construction**.

Most of the risk was expressed through:

- **short flat price exposure**
- **time spread positions**
- **BK exposure** through the WTI-Brent spread

Because flat price moves can be more volatile, the project also used time spreads as a way to express the view in a more structured way when the data lined up.

The platform allowed up to **1,000 contracts of total exposure**, but the project did not go full size immediately. Instead, it left room to scale into positions if needed and kept flexibility for hedging and risk adjustment.

That shows good trading judgment. It is not just about having the right market call. It is also about knowing how much size to put on and how much room to leave for uncertainty.

---

## Risk Management and Position Sizing

A major strength of this project is that it treats **risk sizing** seriously.

The report explains that one CL contract moves about **$1,000 for every $1/bbl move**, which means position size matters a lot. At 800 lots, even a relatively small adverse move can create a large dollar swing.

That is important because it shows awareness of:

- contract mechanics
- exposure scaling
- position concentration
- downside risk in volatile commodity markets

This makes the project stronger than a simple market commentary piece because it connects trade ideas directly to portfolio-level risk.

---

## PnL Attribution

Another useful part of the project is that it breaks down where the profit actually came from.

The report notes that before the penalty:

- the **CL X2025 short** generated the most profit
- the **time spread** added a smaller positive contribution
- **BK** added only a small contribution

This is exactly the kind of thinking recruiters like to see. It is not enough to place trades. You also need to understand which positions really drove the result and which ones mattered less.
